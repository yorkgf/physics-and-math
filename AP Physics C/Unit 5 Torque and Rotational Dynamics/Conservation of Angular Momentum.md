# Conservation of Angular Momentum

**Conservation of Angular Momentum** is one of the most fundamental principles in nature.

## Statement

When the **net external torque** on a system is zero:

$$\vec{L}_{\text{total}} = \text{constant}$$

Or equivalently:

$$\vec{L}_i = \vec{L}_f$$

## Conditions

- $\vec{\tau}_{\text{net, external}} = 0$
- Or when external torque is much smaller than internal torques (approximation)

## Common Applications

### 1. Changing Moment of Inertia

When $I$ changes, $\omega$ changes accordingly to keep $L = I\omega$ constant:

$$I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2$$

### 2. Figure Skating / Diving

Pulling arms/legs inward → $I$ decreases → $\omega$ increases (spin faster)

## Example Problem

**Example:** A figure skater has mass $m = 60$ kg. With arms extended, her moment of inertia is $I_1 = 6$ kg·m² and she rotates with angular velocity $\omega_1 = 2$ rad/s. After pulling her arms in, her moment of inertia becomes $I_2 = 2$ kg·m². Find her final angular velocity.

**Solution:**
$$I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2$$
$$\omega_2 = \frac{I_1\omega_1}{I_2} = \frac{6 \times 2}{2} = 6 \text{ rad/s}$$
